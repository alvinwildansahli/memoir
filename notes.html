Reflections from the Global Sustainable Development Congress: Education, Support, and Meaningful Participation

I have always been enthusiastic about listening to diverse perspectives from different academic, cultural, linguistic, and personal backgrounds...

I have always been enthusiastic about listening to diverse perspectives from different academic, cultural, linguistic, and personal backgrounds. To me, the richness of perspectives creates greater opportunities to generate ideas and innovations with broader impacts. One meaningful opportunity came through my participation in the Global Sustainable Development Congress (GSDC) 2026 in Jakarta, particularly as a Student Delegate in the Higher Education Student Initiative Student Action Group (HESI SAG). This forum brought together students, academics, policymakers, and various stakeholders from around the world to discuss a fundamental question: how global challenges are translated into the unique social, cultural, political, and economic contexts of each country. Through these discussions, we sought to formulate a shared framework that is flexible enough to be adapted across different contexts while maintaining the same objective: advancing sustainable development by positioning students as key actors and young people more broadly as central drivers of change.

One of the most memorable discussions was regarding the importance of integrating sustainability into the education system. Sustainability can no longer be understood solely as an environmental issue, but rather as a framework of thinking that must be present throughout the entire educational process. Climate change, environmental degradation, the energy crisis, social inequality, and technological transformation are interconnected issues that cannot be resolved through a single academic discipline. Therefore, education is required to develop a multidisciplinary approach capable of equipping learners to face the complexities of the future.

However, when I reflected on this discussion within the Indonesian context, a more fundamental question arose: does our education system have sufficient capacity to carry out such a massive agenda?

Many teachers face increasing pressure. They are demanded to foster innovation, creativity, and the modernization of learning, while simultaneously instilling sustainability values in their students. At the same time, the necessary institutional support is often inadequately available. Issues regarding funding, system flexibility, and workloads become recurring obstacles in various discussions.

For example, sustainability education often requires media, methods, or activities that cannot be executed solely through conventional classroom learning. It requires projects, experiments, field observations, or collaborations with various parties. However, specific funding for such activities is not always available. Many educational institutions must reallocate their already limited budgets to support these programs. As a result, the implementation of sustainability often relies on the individual initiatives of teachers or schools, rather than on strong and sustainable systemic support.

A similar situation is felt by school and university students. In various forums, young people are encouraged to innovate, create impact, and become agents of change. In practice, however, the support provided often only appears when success is already visible. Acknowledgement, awards, and recognition are given after a program has been successfully implemented. Yet, the most crucial phase actually occurs long before that: when ideas are being designed, when risks are still high, and when uncertainty still dominates.

During those stages, what is needed is not merely appreciation, but tangible support in the form of funding, expert mentoring, facility access, administrative ease, and the space to experiment and learn from failure. Many youth initiatives stall not due to a lack of creativity, but because of minimal support during the initial phases of idea development.

Another issue relates to the educators' capacity to teach sustainability. In one session, it was revealed that more than 40 percent of educators do not yet feel confident integrating sustainability issues into their teaching. This hesitation arises because many subjects are considered to have no direct connection to environmental or sustainable development themes. Teachers of mathematics, statistics, history, and other fields often struggle to find common ground between the materials they teach and sustainability issues.

Yet, that is exactly where the greatest challenge and opportunity for future education lie. Sustainability does not have to be taught as a separate subject. It can be present through the learning context. Mathematics can discuss carbon emission and energy consumption data. Statistics can teach disaster risk analysis and social inequality. History can explain how resource exploitation shapes the development of human civilization. In other words, sustainability requires not only relevant learning texts but also a context capable of connecting knowledge with the reality of life.

An equally interesting discussion was about the future beyond 2030. The Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) agenda has been the global development framework for more than a decade. However, the question that arises is: what will happen after 2030? Have these goals been successfully achieved? Or does the world actually need a new agenda that is more adaptive to continuously evolving challenges?

This question brought forth diverse perspectives from participants from various countries. I had the opportunity to discuss with students from Cadet College Hasan Abdal, Pakistan. They emphasized that sustainability must not be understood solely as the pursuit of economic growth or physical development. What is equally important is building community resilience in the face of various increasingly frequent crises, ranging from climate change to geopolitical uncertainty.

Another perspective came from colleagues at The Education University of Hong Kong (EdUHK). They raised an issue that has become increasingly relevant in recent years: the relationship between the development of artificial intelligence and energy consumption. Amidst the optimism surrounding AI as a solution to various problems, there are environmental consequences that often escape attention. The computing infrastructure supporting the development and use of AI requires massive amounts of energy. Therefore, the future challenge lies not only in how to develop increasingly advanced technology but also in how to ensure that this technology becomes more efficient and sustainable.

The discussion then evolved into the issue of inequality between developed and developing countries. For many nations in the Global South, including Indonesia, economic development still requires access to cheap, stable, and reliable energy. On the other hand, demands for a transition to clean energy continue to strengthen in various international forums.

In this context, sustainability is often not as simple as choosing between economic development and environmental protection. Many developing countries still face fundamental challenges in the form of limited energy access and infrastructure capacity. Indonesia, for instance, still has a much lower per capita electricity consumption compared to several neighboring countries. Meanwhile, the transformation toward a technology-based and electrified economy requires an increasingly larger energy supply. This dilemma is what makes the sustainability discourse always fraught with compromises, negotiations, and considerations of justice.

Among these various discussions, one thing that continuously emerged was the position of young people in decision-making. Almost everyone agreed that the younger generation is the primary stakeholder for the future of sustainability. Yet, a more critical question is whether that involvement is truly meaningful.

On many occasions, the voices of young people are indeed given space. We are invited to speak, express our aspirations, and even sit in various discussion forums. However, a space to speak does not always mean a space to influence decisions. Not infrequently, youth participation stops at a symbolic level: present as representation, but lacking real influence on the direction of policies or program implementation.

Therefore, the challenge that needs to be addressed moving forward is not merely how to increase youth participation, but how to ensure that this participation has real consequences. The education sector, governments, and various institutions need to move beyond symbolism toward mechanisms that allow the ideas of young people to be genuinely accommodated, tested, and realized.

Ultimately, the experience of attending this forum made me realize that sustainability is not solely an issue of technology, environment, or policy. It is also a matter of intergenerational relations, power distribution, and the courage to build systems that support change. We often ask teachers to innovate without providing sufficient support. We ask university students to create impact without providing a safe space to experiment. We invite young people to talk about the future, but are not necessarily willing to hand over a portion of the decision-making space to them.

Perhaps this is the great work that still needs to be completed after 2030. Not just formulating a new agenda, but ensuring that those who will live the longest with the consequences of that agenda are genuinely involved in the process of shaping it.

Sumur Jalatunda and How Communities Preserve Knowledge

A few months ago, I had the opportunity to participate in a cultural exchange program with friends from Japan in a village in Pasir Eurih, Bogor Regency, West Java...

A few months ago, I had the opportunity to participate in a cultural exchange program with friends from Japan in a village in Pasir Eurih, Bogor Regency, West Java. The main objective of the activity was actually quite simple: to introduce Sundanese culture and rural community life to participants coming from outside Indonesia. Yet, as with many journeys, the most memorable lessons often do not come from the planned main agenda.

One of the places we visited was a well known to the local community as Sumur Jalatunda. According to developing local lore, this well is connected to King Siliwangi and his wife, Dewi Mayang Kentring Manik, believed to have been their resting place in the past. Furthermore, the community believes that this well never dries up, even when facing prolonged dry seasons, El Niño, or various other extreme weather conditions.

As someone accustomed to observing the environment from a more empirical perspective, I was naturally intrigued by this claim. Not out of a desire to prove the stories right or wrong, but because I am always curious about why certain narratives can survive for so long within a community. Upon observing the area around the well, I found something interesting: surrounding Sumur Jalatunda are dense and relatively well-preserved bamboo groves.

The local community holds a strong belief that these bamboo groves must not be cut down indiscriminately. They believe that if the bamboos are felled, the guardian of the well will be angered and various misfortunes could occur. Initially, I considered this story simply as part of the folklore commonly found across different regions. But the longer I observed, the more I felt there was something fascinating behind the relationship between the well and the bamboo.

Simple questions then began to emerge in my mind. Why must it be bamboo that is protected? Why not other trees? Why is the story of the well's guardian always associated with the presence of the surrounding bamboo groves? This curiosity drove me to look up several references regarding the ecological relationship between bamboo and water sources.

It turns out that various studies show bamboo possesses quite unique ecological characteristics. Its root system is capable of increasing water infiltration into the soil, helping to maintain slope stability, reducing erosion, and contributing to the retention of groundwater availability. In some cases, the presence of bamboo groves is also known to help maintain water quality by filtering various materials carried by surface runoff.

At this point, I began to see something compelling. Perhaps communities in the past did not explain this relationship using terms like infiltration, groundwater conservation, groundwater recharge, or ecosystem services. They did not possess the academic language used in scientific journals today. However, that does not mean they did not understand the relationship. They understood that bamboo and water have an inextricably close connection. They understood that felling bamboo could disrupt the sustainability of the water source. They understood that the area around the well had to be protected.

It is just that this knowledge was conveyed in a different form. Instead of stating that the bamboo root system plays a role in maintaining groundwater storage capacity, they said that the well's guardian would be angry if the bamboo was cut down. Scientifically, these two statements are certainly different. But in terms of social function, both produce almost identical behaviors. The community preserves the bamboo. The community protects the water source. The community sustains the well.

At a certain point, I started to wonder whether some of the folklore passed down through generations is actually another form of preserving ecological knowledge. Perhaps the community did not write it down in journals. Perhaps they did not compile it into research reports. But they preserved it in the form of stories, myths, taboos, and beliefs that could be inherited from one generation to the next.

Another question arose when we tried drinking the water from Sumur Jalatunda. The local community holds a belief that the taste of the water a person experiences depends on their inner state. If someone arrives with ill thoughts, the water will taste unpleasant, bitter, or unrefreshing. Conversely, if someone arrives with a calm heart and clear mind, the water will taste fresh, light, and even sweeter. Frankly, I was not entirely convinced by this explanation. I tried drinking the water a few times and found no significant change in taste; it tasted relatively the same as before. Yet, some others claimed to experience something different.

From here, I began to think about other possibilities. Is this a form of suggestion? When someone is told that the taste of the water will change according to their inner state, does the brain then unconsciously begin to seek and construct that experience? Or is there an actual change in the water's chemical composition caused by certain environmental factors? Or perhaps both are at play simultaneously? I do not have a definitive answer. But what intrigues me is not the factual accuracy of the story.

What is more fascinating is why such stories persist. Perhaps the function of the story is not to precisely explain the chemical properties of water. Perhaps the story serves as a reminder that the way we perceive the world is often influenced by our own internal state. When a person is angry, anxious, or burdened, the world does indeed often feel different compared to when they are calm and at peace. It is not just the water that tastes different. Sometimes, the entirety of life's experience feels different.

The longer I thought about the experience at Sumur Jalatunda, the more I felt that traditional societies often store knowledge in a form entirely different from what is commonly used in the academic world. Modern science stores knowledge in the form of data, numbers, graphs, experiments, and publications. Traditional societies store it in the form of stories, symbols, myths, and beliefs. Both utilize different languages. Both operate through different mechanisms. Yet occasionally, upon closer inspection, both are actually attempting to explain the very same phenomenon.

Perhaps that is what makes this experience so profound. That behind a story about King Siliwangi, a well guardian, and bamboo groves that must not be felled, there might be a hidden ecological understanding that has been cultivated for hundreds of years. And perhaps behind the belief that the taste of water depends on the drinker's inner state, lies a simple reminder: how humans view the world is often determined not only by the world itself, but also by their own state of being when viewing it.

Because of this, every time I hear a folktale, I become increasingly reluctant to hastily categorize it as right or wrong. Sometimes, what is more interesting is not whether the story is factual, but rather what knowledge is being preserved and passed down through that story over centuries.

At the Crossroads After Graduation: Education, Employment, and the Crisis of Hope Amidst Uncertainty

Lately, approaching the end of my university years, I have started to think about something I haven't really considered seriously over the past few years...

Lately, approaching the end of my university years, I have started to think about something I haven't really considered seriously over the past few years. The question is simple, but when pondered more deeply, it is remarkably heavy: what will I do after I graduate?

This question might sound ordinary. Almost every final-year student has faced it. Interestingly, however, during my first, second, and even most of my third year of college, that question didn't heavily burden my mind. I was busy living life as a student. There were lectures, organizations, research, assignments, discussions, and various other activities. The future still felt distant enough not to be thought about too seriously.

Because I wasn't looking for it, information regarding job vacancies, recruitment requirements, workplace competition, and various career issues rarely appeared before me. But upon entering the final semester, everything changed. Social media algorithms began displaying job vacancy information. Friends started talking about their first jobs, scholarships, further studies, and various future plans. Slowly, I realized that after years of living on a relatively clear path, I was approaching a crossroads without definite directional signs.

At that point, anxiety began to emerge.

Initially, I thought this anxiety was merely related to personal matters. Am I ready to work? Can I compete? Will I choose the right path after graduation? But the longer I pondered it, I realized that this anxiety didn't entirely originate from myself. It was also born from how I view the education system, the job market, economic conditions, and the ongoing social realities around me.

The more I observed, the more I felt that the issue is much larger than simply finding a first job.

One of the questions that bothers me the most is regarding certification. If universities indeed have the function of producing competent graduates, then why does the professional world still require so many additional certifications?

Hasn't someone spent four years of their life studying at a university? Haven't they completed assignments, practicums, research, a thesis, and various other forms of academic evaluation? Haven't they invested a considerable amount of time, energy, and money to obtain a degree? Then why, upon entering the workforce, are they once again asked to present various additional certificates as proof that they are worthy of consideration?

On one hand, I understand that companies need a way to assess someone's competence. But on the other hand, I cannot ignore the impression that certification has evolved into its own industry. Short training sessions of a few days or weeks can yield certificates that then hold an important position in the recruitment process. In some cases, these certificates even seem easier to read and more valued than a formal education process that lasted for years.

From this, a more fundamental question arises. Have certifications become important because universities have failed to carry out their functions? Or have certifications become important because the professional world no longer fully trusts universities as guarantors of competence? Perhaps both are true to a certain degree.

Universities produce thousands of graduates every year with vastly diverse qualities. On the flip side, companies require indicators that are fast, simple, and easy to compare among applicants. Certifications then step in as an additional signal deemed capable of answering this need.

However, the problem does not stop there. When more people hold degrees, degrees lose some of their distinguishing power. When more people hold certificates, certificates also lose their distinguishing power. Thus, other requirements emerge. Work experience. Portfolios. Competitions. Achievements. Personal branding. Professional networks.

The requirements continue to pile up without ever truly coming to an end. What happens is not the replacement of one requirement with another, but rather the continuous accumulation of requirements. This phenomenon leads me to think that the issue is no longer about competence, but about credential inflation. Each generation is required to have more evidence to show they deserve the same opportunities.

The anxiety becomes even more palpable when observing various contradictions in the professional world. It is not uncommon to find job vacancies explicitly aimed at fresh graduates, which simultaneously require one to two years of work experience. Such contradictions seem simple, but they actually harbor quite serious issues. How can someone gain experience if the very first opportunity to work requires the experience they do not yet possess?

I often feel that parts of the modern recruitment system are built on assumptions that are not entirely logical. On one hand, companies complain about a lack of work-ready talent. On the other hand, they are not always willing to provide space for those who are learning to become professionals. Here, I begin to see that the problem of the workforce is not only related to individual capability. There are structural aspects that cannot be easily dismissed.

The same thing appears when discussing meritocracy. In theory, we are often taught that hard work, competence, and achievement will lead a person to a better life. This narrative is very strong in the world of education. Study well. Improve your skills. Develop yourself. Then opportunities will come.

I am not saying that this narrative is entirely false. However, I also cannot say that this narrative is entirely true. The reality I observe shows that access to opportunities is not always distributed equally. There are other factors working behind the scenes. Social relations, professional networks, family connections, recommendations, and various other forms of social capital exert a non-trivial influence.

For some, these networks have been available from the start. They grow up in environments that have access to the professional world. They know people who are already in certain positions. They have references, information, and support that are not always available to everyone. Conversely, for many first-generation higher education students, the situation is different.

We do not have an inherited map. We do not have paths that were paved beforehand. We are entering a territory that is relatively new for our own families. Therefore, when discussing meritocracy, I always feel the need to be careful. Because competence is indeed important, but competence never stands alone. It always interacts with the various forms of social capital a person possesses.

Another issue that frequently arises is regarding the economic value of higher education itself. I once heard a rather simple question that is difficult to answer. If someone who graduates from high school or vocational school can work directly with a certain income, and then after four years of college someone earns an income that is not much different, what exactly is the added value of higher education?

This question is not always comfortable to discuss. Education is often viewed as something so noble that its economic benefits seemingly cannot be questioned. Yet for many families, education is also a massive investment. There are costs incurred. There is time sacrificed. There are hopes planted. Thus, the question of the relationship between education and economic welfare cannot simply be ignored.

But the more I think about it, the more I realize that the issue is not as simple as comparing the first salary after graduation. Education might not always provide huge benefits at the starting point. But it often influences the trajectory of life in the long run. The issue then shifts. Is that trajectory still strong enough to promise real social mobility for today's youth?

At this point, I begin to see the connection between educational issues and broader economic issues. Indonesia has thousands of universities that produce an enormous number of graduates every year. But the question is, are the available job opportunities truly capable of absorbing them? Even if they are available, do those jobs align with the competencies learned during their education?

This question brings me to the issue of the relationship between universities and industries. Some parties argue that education must adapt to the needs of the labor market. Study programs that are irrelevant to industry needs are considered in need of evaluation, or even elimination. This view has its own logic. However, I also see considerable risks within it.

If a university is only measured by its ability to produce a workforce, then the university slowly transforms into a labor factory. Yet, education has a broader function than merely meeting market needs. Universities are not merely tasked with producing workers. Universities are also tasked with producing human beings who are capable of thinking, understanding reality, asking questions, and developing knowledge.

Here is where I begin to question the way we view human beings. Are humans 'human capital' whose existence is primarily intended to support economic growth? Or do humans possess value that transcends their economic function? This question might sound philosophical, but it actually has very tangible implications. How we answer it will determine how we design education, the job market, and even public policy.

Amidst these various questions, I am also confronted with a more personal issue. I believe there are aspects of life that lie beyond human control. In simple terms, I believe that destiny plays a role in the journey of a person's life. However, this belief does not automatically eliminate the anxiety. Instead, a unique tension arises.

If the future is predetermined, why should I be so worried? But if the future depends entirely on my choices, why do so many factors beyond my control also determine its final outcome? I have not found a completely satisfying answer. All I have found is the realization that humans live between effort and uncertainty. We are required to plan the future without ever truly knowing how the future will take shape.

Perhaps this is what makes the transition period leading up to graduation feel so heavy. For years, life seemed to have a fairly clear path. There was the next semester. There was the next course. There was the next academic target. After graduation, that path disappears. Suddenly, we must determine our own direction.

Yet, my anxiety, it turns out, does not stop at personal issues. The more I observe social conditions, the harder it is for me to separate my personal future from the future of society as a whole. We live in a situation full of paradoxes. We are asked to have hope, ambition, and optimism. We are asked to believe that the future can be built through hard work. But at the same time, the reality we see every day often moves in the opposite direction.

Economic uncertainty, recurring corruption, weak legal certainty, unpredictable political dynamics, pressure on public purchasing power, and various other problems present questions that are not easily answered. How does one maintain hope when the various indicators they observe actually point to uncertainty?

I realize that no country is capable of providing absolute guarantees to all its citizens. No system is perfect. No society is completely free from problems. But still, there is a difference between reasonable uncertainty and uncertainty that continuously erodes trust.

At this point, I begin to understand that a portion of my anxiety is not solely personal anxiety. It is the anxiety of an individual trying to understand their position amidst broader social changes. It is the anxiety of someone who still wants to hope, but is no longer capable of hoping naively. It is the anxiety of someone who still believes in the importance of education, but is beginning to question the relationship between education and the reality its graduates face. It is the anxiety of someone who still believes in hard work, but realizes that hard work does not always operate in a completely fair arena.

Perhaps there is no final conclusion to this entire reflection. Perhaps there shouldn't be one. Because some questions in life do not exist to be answered immediately. Some questions exist to continuously accompany a person on their journey of understanding the world and themselves.

All I understand tonight is one thing. The anxiety approaching graduation is apparently not just the fear of a first job. It is the intersection of personal hopes, social realities, the education system, economic conditions, and questions regarding a broader future.

And perhaps, growing up does not mean successfully eliminating all that anxiety. Perhaps growing up is learning to keep moving forward despite realizing that not all questions will receive definite answers.

Unequal Shelter in a Warming City: A Brief Notes on Urban Heat Adaptation in Jakarta

When discussing urbanization in recent decades, the conversation cannot be separated from the social structures formed within urban spaces. Cities are often perceived as spaces offering various opportunities, yet they are not entirely ideal for all their residents...

When discussing urbanization in recent decades, the conversation cannot be separated from the social structures formed within urban spaces. Cities are often perceived as spaces offering various opportunities and advantages compared to rural areas. Access to education, healthcare, infrastructure, employment, and public facilities is generally more concentrated in urban areas. However, various observations and studies show that the city is not an entirely ideal space for all its residents.

In the perspective of spatial justice proposed by Edward Soja, the distribution of benefits and risks within urban spaces often occurs unevenly. Space is not merely a container for human activity, but it also reflects power relations, resource distribution, and social inequalities developing in society. As a result, some groups gain greater access to various facilities and services, while others bear higher environmental risks.

This phenomenon can be observed in many major cities, including Jakarta. Various strategic facilities such as educational centers, healthcare services, business districts, and urban infrastructure are generally concentrated in specific areas. Conversely, peripheral areas often face greater environmental pressures. Amid the increasing impacts of climate change, this condition becomes even more crucial to address because the communities with the most limited access to resources are often located in areas with the highest vulnerability.

One increasingly apparent form of environmental pressure is the Urban Heat Island (UHI) phenomenon, which is the increase in temperature in urban areas compared to surrounding regions due to the dominance of built surfaces, high human activity, and lack of vegetation.

This temperature increase is not felt evenly by all city residents. Areas with limited public facilities, poor environmental quality, and vulnerable socioeconomic conditions tend to receive a greater impact. In Jakarta, areas with high vulnerability are often associated with densely populated regions dominated by lower-middle-income groups.

The characteristics of these areas are generally marked by relatively low housing quality, limited green open space, inadequate access to public facilities, and proximity to various pollution sources such as industrial zones, main transportation routes, waste treatment plants, and landfills. The combination of these factors amplifies the environmental pressure that communities must endure on a daily basis.

Spatial Exploration: Reading the Patterns in Jakarta

This piece attempts to explore the relationship between the distribution of surface temperatures, population density, building structures, and green open spaces in the Jakarta area through the following series of spatial mappings.

Analysis and Key Findings

This analysis is built upon several basic assumptions. First, high-rise buildings have the ability to create a shading effect that can reduce direct heat exposure on surrounding surfaces. Second, tall buildings are generally associated with the availability of supporting facilities such as air conditioning, a more stable water supply, and relatively better environmental management.

Conversely, areas dominated by low-rise buildings are often associated with densely populated settlements that have limited open spaces and high human activity, potentially increasing local environmental temperatures.

Observations show a fairly strong spatial correlation between population density and the distribution of surface temperatures. High-density centers tend to associate with areas experiencing greater heat stress. At the same time, the distribution of tall buildings and green open spaces also shows an uneven pattern. This condition indicates that environmental benefits and risks in urban areas are not distributed equally.

Some community groups have a better capacity to adapt to rising temperatures, while others face higher vulnerability due to limited resources and the environmental conditions of their neighborhoods. These findings show that the Urban Heat Island problem is not merely a climate or environmental issue, but also a matter of social and spatial justice.

When temperature increases are felt more heavily by community groups with low adaptive capacity, the impacts of climate change have the potential to widen pre-existing inequalities.

End of Note

Therefore, climate change adaptation policies in urban areas need to be directed not only at efforts to lower environmental temperatures, but also at reducing the inequality of vulnerability between regions. Increasing green open spaces, providing equitable public infrastructure, developing heatwave protection facilities, and enhancing the community's adaptive capacity are crucial steps to building a more resilient and just city.

In this context, urban resilience is measured not only by the city's ability to face climate change, but also by the extent to which protection is provided to the community groups most vulnerable to its impacts.
